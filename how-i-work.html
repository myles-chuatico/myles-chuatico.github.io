How I Think

A few things that are
consistently true about me.

01
I build, I don't just document.
When I find a gap, my instinct is to close it — not write a report about it. The eLinq pipeline, the mortgage decision tool, the MPR automation suite: none of those were assigned to me. I saw something that didn't work and built the fix.
02
I learn what the problem needs.
I'm not a coder. But I've written batch scripts, built Power Query pipelines, and shipped a full-stack JavaScript tool. I don't let not knowing something be a reason not to try. I pick up what I need to solve the problem in front of me.
03
I care about accuracy.
I don't claim things I didn't do. When something I built was partially someone else's work, I'll tell you. When a metric is real but unverifiable, I'll flag it. I'd rather undersell and be accurate than oversell and get caught out in an interview.
04
Process rigor isn't optional.
Everything I deploy goes through UAT before production. I document with checklists, screenshots, and audit trails — not because someone told me to, but because I've seen what happens when you skip it. Discipline in the build prevents incidents in production.
05
I work best when given a problem, not a prescription.
Tell me what's broken or what's needed. I'll figure out how to get there. I'm most effective when I have ownership of a problem end-to-end — from understanding the gap, through building the solution, to seeing it stable in production. I don't need to be handed a method. I need to understand the goal.

I've worked across both without friction. I document well enough that async handoffs don't break things, and I communicate clearly enough in person that meetings don't drag. Format doesn't matter much — clarity does.

Toastmasters Presentation Mastery Level 3 — not just a line on a resume. I've presented utilization findings to AVPs, walked leadership through platform KPIs, and facilitated stakeholder reviews. I can communicate technical work to non-technical audiences.

My Process

How a typical initiative
goes for me.

1
Notice the gap
Something is slow, broken, missing, or invisible. Usually it's a process that relies on someone remembering to do it manually, or a question leadership can't answer because the data doesn't exist yet.
2
Understand it properly
I talk to the people actually doing the work — agents, team leads, whoever touches the process day-to-day. I want to know what actually happens, not what the SOP says happens. Then I talk to whoever owns the outcome to understand what "fixed" looks like.
3
Build something that works
I start with what I know, pick up what I don't, and get a working version. It doesn't have to be perfect — it has to work and be testable. I build in UAT first, always.
4
Test it properly
UAT isn't a checkbox. I test it from the user's perspective, edge cases included. I document everything with screenshots. I get stakeholder sign-off before anything goes near production.
5
Ship it and watch it
Production rollout follows the checklist. Post-deployment I monitor closely — first few days especially. If something breaks, I want to catch it before anyone else does.
6
Document and hand off cleanly
Work instruction manuals, agent guides, knowledge base articles — if I built it, I document it well enough that someone else can run it without me. The goal is never to be the single point of failure.
